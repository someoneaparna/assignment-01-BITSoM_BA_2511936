## Storage Systems

I would use a hybrid architecture with purpose-built storage for each hospital goal. For readmission-risk prediction, I would store historical patient encounters, diagnoses, medications, lab summaries, and discharge outcomes in a governed analytical platform such as a cloud data warehouse or lakehouse. That gives the data science team consistent, historical features for model training. For plain-English patient-history search, I would keep the transactional patient record in the core hospital system, but I would also create chunked clinical-note embeddings in a vector database so doctors can ask semantic questions over prior notes, summaries, and discharge documents. For monthly management reporting, I would use the warehouse or lakehouse again because occupancy, cost, and departmental KPI reporting are classic OLAP workloads. For ICU vitals, I would ingest high-frequency telemetry into a time-series or streaming-optimized store first, then land curated aggregates into the analytical platform for longer-term reporting and model features.

## OLTP vs OLAP Boundary

The OLTP boundary ends at the operational systems that support day-to-day care: patient registration, appointments, admissions, orders, medication administration, and clinician updates. These systems need low-latency writes, strict consistency, and audited transactions. The OLAP boundary begins once data is replicated or streamed out of those operational systems into pipelines for downstream analytics. At that point, the focus changes from recording individual clinical transactions to combining and transforming data for reporting, machine learning, and semantic retrieval. The vector database sits just beyond the OLTP boundary because it is derived from clinical documents rather than being the source of truth itself.

## Trade-offs

One significant trade-off is architectural complexity. Using a transactional database, a warehouse or lakehouse, a vector database, and a time-series store provides a strong fit for each workload, but it also creates synchronization, governance, and operational overhead. Data freshness can drift if pipelines fail, and clinicians may lose trust if analytical views do not match the live system closely enough. I would mitigate this by defining one authoritative source for each domain, using event-driven data movement with monitoring and lineage, and publishing freshness SLAs for every downstream system. That way, the design keeps its workload-specific strengths without turning into an uncontrolled sprawl of partially trusted copies.
